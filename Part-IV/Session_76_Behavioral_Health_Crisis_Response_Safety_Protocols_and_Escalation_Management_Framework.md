### SECTION 3: OPENING GROUNDING & RGP (10 minutes)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“Welcome to Session 76. This session is called *Crisis, Suicide Risk, and Staying Alive*. Before we go anywhere, I want to name something directly: **This session is not about making you promise to stay alive.** It’s not about guilt, or shame, or ‘reasons to live.’ It’s about understanding *why* your nervous system might produce the thought of disappearing – and what that thought actually is.

A thought is not a plan. A thought is not a moral failure. A thought is data.

Today, we’re going to look at that data through the lens of your nervous system. We’ll talk about self‑harm as a regulation strategy – not a good one, but a logical one. We’ll talk about why some people develop the capacity to attempt suicide when others don’t. And we’ll leave with a written safety reference card that belongs to you – not a form you fill out for a doctor.

**Here’s what this session is not:** It is not therapy. I am not your clinician. If at any point you notice yourself going somewhere dark, or if you hear someone else say something that worries you, we have a protocol. I will check in privately. No one will be put on the spot.

Now let’s land in the body.”

**RGP exercise – 5 minutes:**
“Close your eyes or lower your gaze. Take a breath in for 4 counts… hold for 2… out for 6. Again. In 4, hold 2, out 6. One more time.

Now just notice. Without changing anything – just notice what you feel. Is there tension somewhere? A heaviness? A numbness? An urge to move?

**Pause 6 seconds.**

If you noticed dorsal stillness – that shut‑down, flat, ‘I’m not here’ feeling – that’s one way the nervous system survives overwhelm. If you noticed sympathetic bracing – tight shoulders, shallow breathing, a sense of being wired – that’s another.

Both are valid. Neither is wrong. You don’t have to fix anything right now.

We’re going to do one more round. This time, if you want, try a wall press. Put your hands on the wall beside you, lean in slightly, and push for 5 seconds. Then release. That pressure can help bring you back into the room.

**Facilitator models quietly. No singling out.**

Okay. Open your eyes when you’re ready.”

**Facilitator speaks:**
“Let’s talk about what happens in the body when suicidal ideation shows up.

Most people think suicidal thoughts are a problem of *meaning* – ‘I have no reason to live.’ ‘Nothing matters.’ ‘I’m a burden.’ And those thoughts are real. But they don’t come from nowhere. They come from a nervous system that has been in survival mode for so long that it starts to run out of options.

Here’s the physiology:
When you experience chronic stress – trauma, moral injury, isolation, pain – your sympathetic nervous system (fight/flight) stays on. Your cortisol stays high. Your body is braced for threat that never ends. Over time, that system exhausts. You can’t stay in fight/flight forever.

So the nervous system tries the other extreme: dorsal vagal shutdown. That’s the ‘freeze’ or ‘collapse’ response. It’s the same system a mouse uses when it plays dead. Heart rate drops. Dissociation increases. Energy flatlines.

**Suicidal ideation often lives in the space between those two states** – the oscillation from wired to numb to wired again. And when that oscillation goes on for months or years, the brain starts to look for a permanent off switch. Not because you’re weak. Because your nervous system is exhausted.

**Key clinical point (deliver slowly):** Suicidal thoughts are not a character flaw. They are a symptom of a nervous system that has run out of regulatory options. The goal of treatment – and the goal of this session – is not to shame that symptom away. It is to add more options.

**Pause. Let that land.**

Now let me ask you – just silently, to yourself – when you have had a moment of ‘I can’t do this anymore,’ what was the body feeling that came with it? Was it a buzzing, wired feeling? Or was it a heavy, numb, ‘I’m already gone’ feeling?

**Pause 10 seconds.**

You don’t have to answer out loud. Just notice.”

### SECTION 5: LECTURE 2 – NSSI AS REGULATORY STRATEGY (10 minutes)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“Some people, when they feel that wired or numb state, turn to non‑suicidal self‑injury – cutting, burning, hitting, scratching. The clinical term is NSSI. And for a long time, the mental health system treated NSSI as manipulative or attention‑seeking. That’s wrong.

**NSSI is a regulation strategy.** A dangerous one, but a logical one.

Here’s why it works – temporarily. When you injure yourself, your body releases endogenous opioids – natural painkillers. That creates a ‘shocking calm.’ The chaos in your head goes quiet for a moment. For someone who has never learned another way to regulate, that moment of quiet can feel like relief.

But there’s a cost.

**Key clinical point (deliver verbatim):**
‘While the goal of NSSI is the ‘shocking calm,’ we must name the clinical reality: recent self‑harm is one of the highest statistical predictors of a future suicide attempt. The nervous system is learning to use pain as a solution, and that is a loop we must interrupt.’

**Pause.**

If you have used NSSI, you are not bad. You are not broken. You found a tool that worked – until it didn’t. Today, we’re going to talk about other tools.

**Group reflection (skip if group size ≤2 or if any participant shows distress):**
‘What’s one thing you’ve done – other than self‑harm – that has ever given you even two seconds of calm?’ You can say it out loud or just think it. Examples: cold water on face, walking, music, punching a pillow, breathing, petting an animal.

**Wait 10 seconds. Do not force responses.**

Now we’re going to talk about the most important tool: creating time and distance between the thought and the action.”

### SECTION 6: MODULE A – MEANS SAFETY EDUCATION (8 minutes – deliver verbatim)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“We’ve talked about why the nervous system reaches for disappearance as a solution. Now let’s talk about what happens in the *window* between that thought and any action.

**Key clinical fact:** Most suicide attempts happen within 10 minutes of the decision point. That means the single most powerful tool you have is *time* and *distance*.

This is not about willpower. This is about physics. If the means are not immediately available, the nervous system has a chance to regulate.

**Three low‑effort, high‑impact actions** (choose one):
1. **Secure firearms** – Use a VA gun lock, store at a friend’s house, or use a lockbox. You don’t have to give up ownership. You just add a 10‑minute barrier.
2. **Medication pause** – If you have a large supply of any medication, give the bottle to someone you trust and keep only a 3‑day supply at home.
3. **Environmental check** – Remove loose sharp objects from easy access. A locked drawer or a different room is enough.

**What this is not:** It is not a judgment. It is not ‘you can’t be trusted.’ It is the same logic as putting a childproof cap on a bottle – your dysregulated brain deserves the same protection as a toddler.

**Facilitator action:** Pass around the means safety materials (gun locks, lockboxes). Say: “If you want one, take one. No questions asked. You can also just look.”

**After distribution, pass out the Means Safety Acknowledgment Log (Section 11).** Say: “Please check what you took – or check ‘I already have a plan.’ This is anonymous. Drop it in the box on your way out.”

### SECTION 7: LECTURE 3 – PERCEIVED BURDENSOMENESS & ACQUIRED CAPABILITY (15 minutes)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“Now we’re going to get into the psychology – the *why* behind the thought ‘people would be better off without me.’

This comes from the Interpersonal Theory of Suicide, developed by Dr. Thomas Joiner. The theory says that suicide happens when two things come together: **perceived burdensomeness** and **thwarted belongingness** – plus a third factor: **acquired capability**.

Let me break that down.

**Perceived burdensomeness** is the belief that your existence is a liability to others – that your family, your unit, your friends would be better off if you were gone. This is not objective fact. This is a symptom. It often overlaps with Moral Injury – the wound that occurs when you believe you have violated your own core moral values. ‘I failed as a father.’ ‘I got my buddy killed.’ ‘I’m a burden on the system.’

**Deliver symptom definition (verbatim):**
‘Perceived burdensomeness (Joiner) often overlaps with Moral Injury — the wound that occurs when you believe you have violated your own core moral values (e.g., ‘I failed as a father’). WRH treats this belief as a symptom of that deep‑level moral conflict.’

**Pause.**

**Thwarted belongingness** is the feeling of being alienated – not belonging to any group, not being understood, not having anyone who would notice if you disappeared. This is common after military service, especially for Veterans who have lost their unit or their mission.

But here’s the critical piece. Many people feel burdensome and alone. Most of them do not attempt suicide. What separates those who attempt from those who don’t is **acquired capability** – the ability to overcome the innate fear of death.

Acquired capability comes from repeated exposure to pain, injury, or violence. Military service, trauma, NSSI, multiple surgeries – these experiences teach the nervous system that pain is tolerable, and that your absence might relieve others. That is a logical conclusion from bad data.

The goal of staying alive is not to argue with that conclusion. It is to add new data. And that’s what the Safety Reference Card is for.”

### SECTION 8: C‑SSRS ADMINISTRATION (10 minutes – insert before Safety Reference Card)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“Before we fill out your safety card, VA policy requires that I ask you a few standard questions. These come from a tool called the Columbia Scale. It’s used in every VA setting to make sure people get the right level of support. I will read each question. Please answer yes or no – just to yourself, or you can tell me privately if you prefer. You do not have to answer out loud to the group.”

**Distribute the C‑SSRS Brief Screener (Section 23).** Read each item aloud. Give participants 5 seconds per item to check a box.

**After collection, say:**
“Thank you. I will review these privately. If any additional support is needed, I will speak with you individually after the group.”

**Facilitator action:** Immediately review each screener. If any “Yes” to questions 1–5 or question 6, **stop the group flow** and activate Section 24 (CSRE Escalation). Do not proceed to the Safety Reference Card until the on‑call clinician has initiated the CSRE.

### SECTION 9: CASE STUDY INTEGRATION (Optional – 5 minutes – skip if group unstable or time short)
**Facilitator speaks (only if group is calm and no recent SPI activations):**
“I want to show you what these ideas look like in a real person’s life. I have permission to share excerpts from a man named Daniel. He wrote a book called *From the Storm to the Fire*. His nervous system was wired the way we’ve been describing – starting in a NICU incubator, then domestic violence, then the deaths of 23 people he loved, then addiction, then suicidal crises.

**Safety note:** I will read only two short passages. If at any point you feel overwhelmed, you can raise your hand and step into the Safety Zone. No explanation needed.

**Read the following excerpts exactly:**
*Excerpt 1 (Perceived Burdensomeness & Freeze):*
“She’d threaten to kick me out, rip the kids from me if I didn’t kneel… I was suicidal, convinced I’d failed our kids forever.”

*Excerpt 2 (Acquired Capability & Ember):*
“My brother was dead at thirty‑two. My mom. Loss stacked on loss… The ember still burns. Fueled by the love for my kids, by the stubborn refusal of that three‑pound preemie to give up.”

**After reading, pause and say:**
“That’s perceived burdensomeness. That’s freeze. That’s the ember. Daniel is not broken. He’s a man whose nervous system learned survival under impossible conditions – and he’s still here.

**You don’t have to share your story. But if any of that felt familiar, you are not alone.** ”

### SECTION 10: SAFETY REFERENCE CARD – GUIDED COMPLETION (15 minutes)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“Take out the card in front of you. It has four sections. We’re going to fill it out together. You do not have to share what you write.

**Section 1: My personal warning signs** – What are the first things you notice when your nervous system is heading toward crisis? Not the big stuff – the small stuff. ‘I stop eating.’ ‘I stop returning texts.’ ‘My shoulders go up.’ ‘I start watching the same dark video over and over.’ Write down three.

**Pause 2 minutes.**

**Section 2: Coping strategies I can use alone** – These are things that don’t require another person. Cold water on your face. Pushing a wall. Walking around the block. Listing five things you can see. Write down three that have worked before – or that you’re willing to try.

**Pause 2 minutes.**

**Section 3: People and places that help** – List two people you could call, even if it’s awkward. List one place you can go that feels safe – a coffee shop, a library, a VA waiting room, a park bench.

**Pause 2 minutes.**

**Section 4: Professional resources** – This is already filled in. 988 Press 1 for the Veterans Crisis Line. 741741 for Crisis Text Line. And the local VA emergency number. Put these in your phone now.

**Pause 2 minutes.**

**Final step:** Turn the card over. Write one sentence – your reason to stay for the next 24 hours. It can be small. ‘My dog needs fed.’ ‘I haven’t finished that show.’ ‘I told my buddy I’d call.’ ‘I don’t want my mom to get that call.’ No one will see this but you.

**Pause 3 minutes.**

**If a participant refuses to write anything:** Say, “That’s okay. Would you be willing to take the card home and fill it out later? The most important part is the crisis numbers – please save those in your phone now.”

**If a participant is distressed but not suicidal:** Say, “Let’s just put the card aside. Can you take three breaths with me? In… out.”

**Visual check:** “If you are comfortable, hold up your card when you’re done. You don’t have to show it. Just a visual check.”

**Facilitator note:** If any participant triggered SPI earlier, sit nearby during this section. Do not isolate them, but maintain proximity.”

### SECTION 13: CLOSING GROUNDING & GROUP CHECK‑OUT (5 minutes)
**Facilitator speaks:**
“We’re coming to the end. Before we go, let’s do one more grounding round.

Feet on the floor. Hands on your thighs. Take a breath in for 4… hold… out for 6. One more time.

Now I’m going to ask each of you – **voluntarily** – to say one word. Just one word that describes how you’re feeling right now. It can be ‘tired,’ ‘okay,’ ‘lighter,’ ‘still heavy.’ No explanations. No fixing. Just the word.

**Facilitator goes around the room. Accept any word. No follow‑up.**

Thank you.

**Do not dismiss yet.** I am going to speak with each of you individually for two minutes before you leave. Please stay seated. While you wait, look at your Safety Reference Card one more time. Put it in your wallet or your phone case. Take it with you.”
